## Executive Summary

Samartya Apps operates in a high-impact, underserved niche: assistive technology for children with language disabilities. Its two products — FreeSpeech (for autism and language disorders) and Dyslexia Reader (for dyslexia) — are research-backed, award-winning, and endorsed by credible institutions (TED, Apple, Madras Dyslexia Association). Samartya recently acquired both products from Avaz Inc. and now operates them independently, creating both an opportunity (clean brand identity) and a challenge (re-establishing awareness under a new name).

This strategy is designed for a lean team (one part-time founder, one engineer, a future marketing hire) and a starting budget of approximately $1,000/month. It prioritizes high-leverage, repeatable, and increasingly automated marketing activities that target the US and Indian markets. The primary objective is to grow awareness among special education decision-makers in US school districts and Indian educational institutions, convert that awareness into app installs and trial usage, and convert trials into paid subscriptions or one-time purchases.

A critical near-term opportunity: the web version of Dyslexia Reader is expected in approximately three months. This unlocks a fundamentally new acquisition channel — web-based trials that require no app install — and the strategy accounts for this throughout.

The strategy is organized around six pillars: (1) establishing baseline data and measurement, (2) foundational positioning and the Avaz-to-Samartya brand transition, (3) a seasonal marketing engine aligned to school purchasing calendars, (4) digital presence and content, (5) partnerships and earned media (with a rethought conference approach for an India-based team), and (6) trial-to-conversion optimization, including the web version launch.

## 1. Establishing Baseline Data: What to Measure Before You Market

You have no current data on installs, traffic, or conversion rates. Before spending a single dollar on marketing, you need baselines. Here is a concrete action plan to establish them, largely using free tools, within the first two weeks.

### 1.1 App Store Data (Free, Built-In)

Both Apple App Store Connect and Google Play Console provide rich analytics at no cost. These should be your primary data source.

**Apple App Store Connect — App Analytics:** Log in to App Store Connect and navigate to App Analytics for each app. Key metrics to capture immediately: Impressions (how many times the app appeared in search results or browse), Product Page Views (how many people tapped to see the full listing), App Units (downloads), Sales and Proceeds (revenue), and Sources (where installs came from — App Store Search, App Store Browse, Web Referral, App Referral). Export the last 12 months of data if available. This will show seasonal patterns, which keywords currently drive impressions, and whether the app's organic discoverability has been stable, growing, or declining since the acquisition from Avaz.

**Google Play Console — Statistics:** Navigate to Statistics for each app. Key metrics: Store listing visitors, Installers (new + returning), Uninstallers, Ratings and Reviews, and Acquisition Reports (which search terms and channels drive installs). Google Play also provides a "Store Listing Experiments" feature for A/B testing different icons, screenshots, and descriptions — this is free and should be used later for optimization.

**Action item:** Export all available historical data from both platforms into a spreadsheet. This becomes your baseline document. Time required: about 2 hours total.

### 1.2 ASO Keyword Research (Free Tools)

To understand where you stand in app store search and where the opportunities are, use these free tools:

**AppFollow (free tier):** Tracks 2 apps with 20 keyword requests. Use it to check current keyword rankings for both FreeSpeech and Dyslexia Reader. Enter your app URLs and see which keywords you currently rank for, and at what position. This immediately tells you what's working and what's not. URL: appfollow.io/free-aso-tools

**ASOTools (free tier):** Provides keyword research, competitor keyword analysis, and download estimates for both iOS and Android. Use the keyword research tool to check search volume and difficulty for your target keywords. Enter competitor apps (Speechify, Voice Dream Reader, Proloquo2Go, Read&Write) to see which keywords drive their traffic. URL: asotools.io

**ASOMobile (16 free tools):** Offers keyword popularity checks, keyword suggestions, competitor keyword spying, category ranking tracking, and more — all free. The "Keyword Check" tool shows popularity and competition for any keyword across both stores. The "Text Analyzer" tool scans any competitor app listing and extracts their keywords. URL: asomobile.net/free-tools

**AppDrift (free plan):** Provides AI-powered metadata generation, keyword tracking for 1 app, and a free screenshot generator. Useful for generating optimized titles, subtitles, and descriptions. URL: appdrift.co

**Google Trends:** Free and requires no account. Use it to compare search interest over time for terms like "dyslexia app," "autism grammar app," "speech therapy app," and "AAC app." This shows seasonality (you'll likely see spikes in September and January) and relative demand.

**Recommended keyword research workflow (one-time, approximately 4 hours):**

Step 1: Use ASOMobile's Keyword Check to evaluate 30–40 candidate keywords for each app. For each keyword, note the popularity score (search volume proxy) and competition score. Prioritize keywords with moderate-to-high popularity and low-to-moderate competition.

Step 2: Use ASOTools to spy on 3–4 competitor apps per product. See which keywords they rank for that you don't — these are gaps to fill.

Step 3: Use AppFollow to check your current rankings for your top 20 target keywords. Record where you rank today.

Step 4: Use AppDrift's AI metadata generator to draft optimized titles, subtitles, and keyword fields (iOS) or descriptions (Google Play) incorporating your target keywords.

Step 5: Implement the changes in App Store Connect and Google Play Console. Track ranking changes weekly using AppFollow or ASOMobile's free Keyword Monitor.

**Candidate keywords to research (starting list):**

For FreeSpeech: "grammar app autism," "language learning autism," "picture sentence builder," "speech therapy app kids," "visual grammar app," "ESL grammar app," "language therapy autism," "AAC grammar," "sentence building app," "special education language app"

For Dyslexia Reader: "dyslexia reading app," "reading app dyslexia kids," "text to speech school," "scan and read aloud," "dyslexia classroom app," "reading support app," "accessible reading app," "dyslexia tools for teachers," "assistive reading app," "reading intervention app"

### 1.3 Website Analytics

If a Samartya Apps website exists, install Google Analytics 4 (GA4) and Google Search Console immediately. GA4 tracks visitor behavior; Search Console shows which Google searches lead people to your site and where you rank. Both are free.

If no website exists yet, this is a Month 1 priority (see Section 3).

### 1.4 Estimating Current Baselines

Without historical data, use these proxies to set reasonable starting estimates:

- **Current monthly installs:** Check App Store Connect and Google Play Console for the last 3 months' average. For niche education apps with minimal active marketing, a typical baseline might be 50–200 installs/month combined.
- **Trial-to-paid conversion rate:** If you don't have this data, industry benchmarks for freemium education apps range from 2–5% for individual users and 10–20% for users who received a personal recommendation or demo. These become your initial assumptions until you have real data.
- **Website traffic:** If a site exists, check GA4 for the last 90 days. If starting from zero, assume 0 and track growth.

## 2. The School Purchasing Calendar: Timing Everything to the Buyer's Clock

This is the single most important strategic insight for Samartya Apps' US marketing. School districts do not buy on your schedule — they buy on theirs. Every marketing activity must be timed to this calendar.

### 2.1 The US School District Fiscal Year and Purchasing Cycle

Most US school districts operate on a July 1 – June 30 fiscal year. The purchasing cycle has distinct phases, and your marketing activities must align to each one:

**Phase 1: Needs Assessment (May–August)**
What happens: As the school year ends, administrators and special education teams assess what worked and what didn't. Building principals submit preliminary budget requests to the district in late April/May. Over the summer, AT specialists and special ed directors review IEPs, evaluate which students need new tools, and begin researching options.

What you should do: This is your awareness-building window. Your content, emails, and social media should be educating decision-makers about the problems your apps solve. Don't sell — inform. Publish blog posts about evidence-based approaches to grammar intervention for students with autism, or how to support independent reading for dyslexic students. The goal is to be on their radar when they start their fall evaluations.

Important caveat: Schools are shut for summer. Teachers and SLPs are largely unreachable June–August. But AT specialists and directors of special education often work year-round and use the summer to research. Target these roles specifically during summer months.

**Phase 2: Information Gathering and Evaluation (September–November)**
What happens: School is back in session. IEP teams are meeting, identifying students who need AT, and actively seeking solutions. This is when SLPs and special educators trial apps, attend conferences (ATIA, Closing the Gap), and request product demos. Budget discussions for the coming fiscal year are not yet happening, but current-year discretionary funds may be available.

What you should do: This is your heaviest outreach period. Launch email campaigns, offer free evaluation copies, host webinars, publish case studies. Every piece of marketing should make it easy for an evaluator to try the app with a student and share results with their team. This is also when Dyslexia Awareness Month (October) provides a natural content hook for Dyslexia Reader.

**Phase 3: Budget Planning and Proposals (January–March)**
What happens: The most impactful budget discussions happen in this window. About 40% of district administrators say March–April is when the most critical budget add/cut decisions are made, and another 32% say January–February. Special education directors who trialed your app in the fall are now deciding whether to include it in next year's budget request.

What you should do: Follow up with every evaluator who received a trial copy in the fall. Provide them with the materials they need to make an internal case: a one-page administrator's guide, VPP pricing details, outcome data from their trial or from case studies, and FERPA/COPPA compliance documentation. Make it easy for them to write your app into a purchase order or budget line item.

**Phase 4: Budget Approval and Purchasing (April–June)**
What happens: Budgets are finalized and approved by school boards (typically April–May). Purchase orders are issued. There is often a "use it or lose it" spending rush in May–June as districts spend remaining current-year funds before the June 30 fiscal year end. June–July see the highest volume of EdTech purchase orders.

What you should do: This is closing season. Send final reminders about VPP pricing. Offer to process quotes quickly. Be responsive to purchase order requests — procurement teams are processing many orders simultaneously and will skip vendors who are slow. The May–June rush is also a good time to reach out to districts that didn't buy during their primary cycle but may have leftover funds.

**Dead zone: July–August.** Do not waste marketing budget on outreach during this period. Staff are on vacation, emails go unread, and decision-makers are not in purchasing mode. Use this time for internal work: content creation, website improvements, ASO optimization, and preparing for the September push.

### 2.2 The India School Calendar

Indian schools generally follow an April–March academic year (with some states and school types varying). Key timing:

**April–May: Summer break.** Schools are closed. Not a good time for outreach to schools, but useful for reaching parents directly (parents often research tools during breaks).

**June–July: New academic year begins.** This is the Indian equivalent of the US September window. Schools are assessing new students, identifying children who need support, and making purchasing decisions. MDA's school outreach programs are typically active during this period. Coordinate marketing efforts with MDA's schedule.

**October–November: Mid-year assessment.** Schools evaluate student progress. Teachers and resource room coordinators may identify additional needs for AT tools. Dyslexia Awareness Month (October) provides a content hook.

**January–March: Academic year wind-down.** Examinations dominate. This is a poor period for school outreach but can be used for parent-directed marketing ("prepare for next year").

## 3. Foundational Positioning and the Avaz-to-Samartya Brand Transition

### 3.1 Managing the Brand Transition

Samartya Apps recently purchased FreeSpeech and Dyslexia Reader from Avaz Inc. This is a clean break — there is no ongoing relationship. This creates both an opportunity and a challenge.

**The opportunity:** You can build a focused brand identity around Samartya Apps as a specialist in language and reading apps for children with disabilities. You're no longer a sub-product of a larger AAC company; you're the company.

**The challenge:** Existing brand equity (App Store reviews, web links, the TED talk, Apple features) all reference "Avaz FreeSpeech" and "MDA Avaz Reader." Users, therapists, and educators who know the products know them by their old names. Abruptly changing everything risks losing discoverability and confusing existing users.

**Recommended transition approach:**

Phase 1 (Months 1–3): Introduce Samartya Apps as the new home. App Store listings should read "FreeSpeech — by Samartya Apps (formerly Avaz FreeSpeech)" and "Dyslexia Reader — by Samartya Apps (formerly MDA Avaz Reader)." This preserves keyword discoverability for people searching the old names while introducing the new brand. Update the developer name in App Store Connect and Google Play Console to "Samartya Apps."

Phase 2 (Months 4–6): Gradually reduce the "formerly" references. The app names become simply "FreeSpeech" and "Dyslexia Reader" with "by Samartya Apps" as the developer attribution. All new marketing materials use the Samartya Apps brand exclusively.

Phase 3 (Months 7–12): The transition is complete. "Avaz" references remain only in historical context (e.g., "The technology behind FreeSpeech was originally developed by Avaz and was the subject of a TED talk...").

**Critical SEO note:** Ensure the new Samartya Apps website includes pages that rank for searches containing "Avaz FreeSpeech" and "MDA Avaz Reader," with clear messaging that these products are now part of Samartya Apps. This captures anyone searching for the old names and redirects them to the new brand.

### 3.2 Brand Narrative and Messaging

**Positioning statement:** "Samartya Apps builds evidence-based language and reading apps for children with learning disabilities. Our products are rooted in original research — including technology featured in a TED talk viewed over 1 million times — and developed in collaboration with clinicians, educators, and organizations with decades of expertise in autism and dyslexia."

**Product-level messaging:**

FreeSpeech: "The only app that teaches grammar through pictures. Children drag and drop visual tiles to build sentences — and FreeSpeech converts them into grammatically correct English. Designed for children with autism, language disorders, and English language learners. Based on technology featured in a TED talk with over 1 million views."

Dyslexia Reader: "Turn any classroom reading material into an accessible reading experience. Scan a page or upload a PDF — Dyslexia Reader provides text-to-speech, syllabification, onset/rime support, and more. Built on 20+ years of dyslexia research by the Madras Dyslexia Association. Available on iOS, Android, and soon on the web."

### 3.3 Website

Create a unified samartyaapps.com website that serves as the authoritative home for both products. Structure:

- Homepage: Mission, both products, credibility markers (TED talk embed, awards, MDA partnership)
- /freespeech: Product page with screenshots, demo video, download links, "For Schools" pricing
- /dyslexia-reader: Product page with screenshots, demo video, download links, "For Schools" pricing, web version waitlist/launch page
- /for-schools: VPP pricing, volume licensing, institutional trial request, administrator's guide downloads, FERPA/COPPA compliance
- /research: TED talk, academic credentials, MDA partnership, any published studies
- /blog: Content marketing hub
- /contact: Inquiry form for institutional buyers

**Budget:** $200–400 one-time for domain, hosting, and a clean WordPress or Webflow template. If using a freelancer for initial setup, budget $500–800.

## 5. Targeted Outreach to School Districts (US)

### 5.1 Building the Prospect List

US school district contact information is publicly available through state education department directories. Key roles to target: AT specialists, directors of special education, SLPs, and AT coordinators.

**Sources:**
- State-by-state special education directories (available on state DOE websites)
- ASHA ProFind directory (individual SLPs)
- LinkedIn searches filtered by title and industry (K–12 Education)
- Conference attendee lists from past ATIA, Closing the Gap, ASHA events (if available)
- Organic sign-ups from your website and content

**Prioritization:** Start with the largest school districts (more students = more need = more budget). The top 500 US school districts by enrollment represent a disproportionate share of AT spending. Then expand to mid-size districts with dedicated AT staff.

**Target:** 2,000 contacts by Month 3, 5,000 by Month 6.

### 5.2 Email Sequence Design

Use Mailchimp (free up to 500 contacts) or ConvertKit. Design a 5-email drip sequence timed to the purchasing calendar:

**Fall Campaign (September launch):**

Email 1 (Day 0): "We'd love to offer you a free trial of FreeSpeech / Dyslexia Reader for your students." Brief product description, TED talk link, promo code for extended free access. Personalize by role (SLP vs. AT specialist vs. director).

Email 2 (Day 5): Educational content. A blog post relevant to their role, with no hard sell.

Email 3 (Day 12): Social proof. Case study or testimonial with a specific outcome data point.

Email 4 (Day 20): Product deep-dive. Short video walkthrough with an invitation to schedule a 15-minute demo or join a webinar.

Email 5 (Day 30): Direct offer. Remind them of the free trial, highlight VPP pricing, provide a link to purchase or request a volume quote.

**Spring Follow-Up Campaign (January launch):**

For contacts who engaged with the fall campaign but didn't convert: budget-focused messaging. "Including FreeSpeech in your AT budget for next year? Here's what you need." Include the administrator's guide PDF, VPP pricing, and an offer to provide a quote for their purchasing department.

**May–June Fiscal Year Close Campaign:**

Short, direct: "End-of-year funds? FreeSpeech and Dyslexia Reader are available via Apple VPP with 50% off for 20+ licenses. We can process your order this week."

### 5.3 Promo Codes and Free Access

Continue offering free copies to evaluators via App Store promo codes. Track every code issued: recipient name, district, date, and follow-up date. A simple Google Sheets tracker is sufficient initially.

**Budget:** $30–50/month for email platform, $0 for promo codes, $100–200/month for list-building tools if needed.

## 6. Rethought Conference Strategy for an India-Based Team

### 6.1 The Cost Reality

With the team based in India, attending US conferences is expensive. A single conference trip involves approximately $1,500–2,000 for flights (India to US), $1,000–1,500 for hotel (3–4 nights), $500–800 for conference registration, and $2,000+ for an exhibit booth. That's $5,000–6,000+ per conference — five to six months of your entire marketing budget. A booth alone exceeds two months of budget. This makes in-person US conference attendance impractical at the current budget level. However, conferences remain the highest-trust channel for AT decision-makers, so you need a smarter approach.

### 6.2 Virtual Conference Participation (High Priority, Low Cost)

ATIA, Closing the Gap, and ASHA all now offer virtual attendance and virtual presentation options at a fraction of in-person costs. ATIA's virtual event, for instance, provides access to 95+ sessions and the ability to present remotely, with CEU credits for attendees.

**Strategy:** Submit session proposals for virtual presentation slots. A well-delivered 45-minute webinar titled "Teaching Grammar Through Pictures: How FreeSpeech Supports Language Goals for Students with Autism" — presented virtually from India — reaches the same professional audience at approximately $150–300 in registration fees instead of $5,000+.

**Priority virtual opportunities:**
- ATIA 2027 Virtual Event (January 2027): Submit a proposal by mid-2026
- Closing the Gap 2026 (October 2026): Check if virtual presentation is available
- ASHA Convention 2026 (November 2026): Submit a poster or virtual session proposal
- Closing the Gap Solutions Webinar Series: Ongoing, accepts external presenters

### 6.3 Leveraging Conference Communities Without Attending

Even without attending in person, you can benefit from conference communities:

**Sponsor a specific element remotely:** Some conferences offer low-cost sponsorship tiers (session sponsor, app of the day, email blast sponsor) that provide brand visibility without requiring physical presence. These can cost $500–1,500 — still significant but more manageable than full attendance.

**Conference social media engagement:** During major conferences, follow the hashtags (#ATIA2027, #CTGConf, #ASHA26), engage with attendees' posts, share relevant content, and offer free trial codes. This creates visibility among exactly the right audience at exactly the right time, at zero cost.

**Post-conference outreach:** Conference attendee lists are sometimes available to sponsors or can be partially reconstructed from social media engagement. After a conference, send targeted emails to attendees: "Missed our session at ATIA? Here's the recording and a free trial code."

**Connect with conference presenters:** Reach out to well-known SLPs, AT specialists, or special educators who present at these conferences. Offer them free access to FreeSpeech or Dyslexia Reader. If they like the product, they become advocates who mention it in their own presentations — far more credible than your own booth.

### 6.4 India-Based Conferences (Low Cost, High Value)

For India, conferences are much more accessible. MDA participates in and hosts events throughout the year. Samartya Apps should co-present with MDA at every opportunity. Other India-based opportunities:

- MDA Annual Conference and training workshops
- National Institute of Mental Health and Neuro-Sciences (NIMHANS) events
- India Disability Foundation conferences
- IIT Madras-hosted education technology events
- ISTE India or EdTechReview events

These require minimal travel cost (domestic India travel) and provide direct access to Indian educators and parents.

### 6.5 The One Annual In-Person US Trip (When Budget Allows)

Once annual revenue justifies it (perhaps Year 2, when the budget increases to $2,000–3,000/month), plan one strategic US trip per year that combines multiple activities:

- Attend one major conference (ATIA or Closing the Gap)
- Schedule 3–5 school district meetings in the same region during the trip
- Record video testimonials with US-based users
- Meet with publication editors (ASHA Leader, etc.) for relationship-building

This maximizes the ROI of a single expensive trip. Budget $4,000–5,000 for this annual trip, reserved across several months of the marketing budget.

## 8. The Dyslexia Reader Web Version: A Strategic Inflection Point

### 8.1 Why the Web Version Changes Everything

Currently, both products require users to install an iOS or Android app. This creates friction: the user must find the app in the store, download it, create an account, and navigate the onboarding flow. For institutional evaluators, this is compounded by device management policies, app approval processes, and IT department gatekeeping.

A web version of Dyslexia Reader eliminates most of this friction. An evaluator can click a link in an email, land on a web page, upload a PDF or scan a page, and experience the core product value in under two minutes — without installing anything, without IT approval, without a specific device type.

This is a fundamental change in the acquisition funnel for Dyslexia Reader, and the marketing strategy should treat it as a major launch event.

### 8.2 Pre-Launch Strategy (Now through Launch)

**Build a waitlist:** Add a "Dyslexia Reader Web — Coming Soon" section to the website immediately. Collect email addresses from anyone interested. This builds a launch-day audience and provides a signal of demand.

**Content marketing:** Publish a blog post: "Why We're Bringing Dyslexia Reader to the Web — And What It Means for Classrooms." Share across social channels and in email campaigns. The message: no more app installs, no more device restrictions, accessible from any browser on any device.

**Prioritize launch timing:** If the web version can be ready by September 2026, that aligns perfectly with the start of the US school evaluation season. If it takes until November, that still catches the bulk of fall evaluations. If it can be accelerated to August, you're ahead of the curve. The September alignment is worth pushing for.

### 8.3 Launch Strategy

**Day 1 launch activities:**
- Email blast to entire contact list (including waitlist)
- Social media announcement across all platforms
- Blog post with full feature walkthrough and embedded demo
- Submit for App Store / review site coverage (including a "now on web" angle)
- Send free access links to 50–100 priority school district contacts with a personal note

**Ongoing web-specific marketing:**
- Google Ads for keywords like "dyslexia reading tool," "classroom reading support dyslexia," "scan and read aloud dyslexia." Web-based products convert much better from search ads than app install ads because there's no friction between the ad click and the product experience. Start with $100–200/month in Google Ads and measure cost-per-trial-start.
- SEO: The web version creates new pages that can rank for target keywords. Each reading support feature (text-to-speech, syllabification, onset/rime) can have its own landing page targeting specific keywords.
- Shareable trial links: Create URLs that evaluators can share with colleagues, administrators, and IEP teams. "Click this link to try Dyslexia Reader with your classroom materials — no install needed."

### 8.4 The Web Version as a Lead Generation Engine

The web version can serve a dual purpose: (1) as a standalone product, and (2) as a top-of-funnel lead generation tool for the mobile apps. A user who tries Dyslexia Reader on the web and finds it valuable is far more likely to install the mobile app for daily classroom use. The web-to-mobile conversion flow should be smooth: after the web trial, prompt users to install the mobile app for offline access, camera-based scanning, and a native experience.

## 9. Trial-to-Conversion Optimization

### 9.1 In-App Onboarding

Both apps need a frictionless first experience that delivers the "aha moment" as fast as possible.

**FreeSpeech:** Guide users to create their first sentence in under 2 minutes. Pre-load 3–5 challenge sets so therapists can assign them immediately without creating content from scratch.

**Dyslexia Reader:** Pre-load 3–5 reading passages at different levels. Make the scan-a-page flow dead simple. Demonstrate text-to-speech and syllabification within the first session.

### 9.2 Trial Nudge Sequence

Day 1: Welcome + quick-start guide.
Day 3: "Have you tried [key feature]?"
Day 7: Progress summary + suggest sharing with a colleague.
Day 10: "4 days left — here's what you keep with a subscription."
Day 13: Final reminder + subscribe button + testimonial.
Day 15 (post-expiry): "We miss you — here's 20% off for 7 days."

### 9.3 Institutional Sales Support

Create a one-page "Administrator's Guide" PDF for each product: problem solved, evidence base, features, pricing (including VPP), FERPA/COPPA compliance. Make this downloadable from the website and attachable to emails.

Offer a 30-day institutional evaluation period (longer than the 14-day individual trial).

Respond to institutional inquiries within 24 hours. A slow response kills deals that took months of awareness-building to generate.

### 9.4 Pricing Clarity and India Pricing

Present pricing with extreme clarity on the website and app stores. Suggested framework: "Free to try for 14 days. Then: $9.99/month, $99.99/year, or [one-time price] for lifetime access. Schools: 50% off with Apple VPP (20+ licenses). Contact us for a volume quote."

For India, set regional pricing in both app stores at 50–70% below US prices. Develop a separate India pricing sheet for schools and NGOs.

## 14. Risk Factors and Mitigations

**Risk: Team bandwidth.** One part-time founder, no marketing hire yet. **Mitigation:** Hire the generalist as the first action. Until then, focus exclusively on ASO optimization (free, one-time effort) and email list building.

**Risk: Brand confusion from Avaz transition.** **Mitigation:** Follow the phased transition plan in Section 3.1. Include "formerly Avaz FreeSpeech" in listings for 3–6 months.

**Risk: Long US sales cycles.** School districts take 6–12 months from first contact to purchase. **Mitigation:** Start outreach now (April–May 2026), even before the website is fully built, so leads are warmed up for September evaluations and spring 2027 purchases.

**Risk: Conference ROI at current budget.** **Mitigation:** Virtual-first conference strategy. No in-person US trips until revenue justifies it. India conferences through MDA partnership.

**Risk: Web version delay.** If Dyslexia Reader web misses September, it misses the strongest US evaluation window. **Mitigation:** Prioritize a minimal viable web version for September, even if some features come later. The core value (upload PDF → get reading supports) must work at launch.

**Risk: Budget constraints.** $1,000/month across two products and two countries is tight. **Mitigation:** Ruthlessly prioritize. Email + SEO + ASO are the highest-ROI channels. Don't spread across too many paid channels. Test one at a time, scale what works.
